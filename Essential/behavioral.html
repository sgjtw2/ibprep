Core Principles
Be specific, not generic
Don't say "I'm hardworking." Say "I rebuilt the entire model overnight when the client changed assumptions at 10pm."
Quantify everything
"Reduced client onboarding time by 40%" beats "significantly improved the process" every time.
Show self-awareness
Interviewers test whether you know why you're a good fit — not just whether you want the job.
Have 5–6 core stories
Each story should be adaptable to multiple questions. One strong leadership story can answer: leadership, conflict, initiative, and failure questions.

MUST PREP Why investment banking? Motivation ▼
What they're really asking
Do you understand what the job actually is? Are you here for the right reasons or just chasing prestige and money?
Strong answer structure
1. The pull (not the push): Explain what attracted you to IB specifically — deal execution, advising companies at inflection points, the intersection of finance and strategy. Don't say "I want to learn a lot" or "the money."

2. A specific trigger: A deal you followed, a person who influenced you, a class or internship that crystallised it.

3. Demonstrated commitment: What have you done to prepare? (Models, networking, this prep app...)
Do
Reference a specific deal or transaction
Mention the analytical rigour you crave
Connect to your long-term goals
Show you know what analysts actually do
Don't
Say "I want to make a lot of money"
Give a generic "passion for finance" answer
Make it sound like a fallback option
Be vague about what IB actually does
MUST PREP Why this bank specifically? Motivation ▼
What they're really asking
Have you done your research? Are you genuinely interested or just spraying applications? Will you turn down our offer for a better one?
Strong answer structure
3-part answer:
1. Something specific about the bank's culture, deal flow, or team (from networking/research — not the website)
2. A deal the bank recently worked on that you found compelling
3. A person you spoke with at the bank and what they told you

Never say "top league table rankings" or "Goldman is the best bank" — that's lazy. Show you dug deeper.
MUST PREP Tell me about yourself / Walk me through your resume. Motivation ▼
Structure: 90-second narrative arc
Present → Past → Future (or reverse for chronological flow)

1. Where you are now (current degree/school, relevant context)
2. Key experience 1 — most relevant role, 2 sentences max
3. Key experience 2 — what you learned, how it connects
4. Why IB + why this bank — the logical conclusion of your story

This is not a resume recitation. It's a story that ends with "and that's why I'm sitting in front of you today."
Common mistakes
Going chronologically and reading your resume out loud. Starting with high school. Not connecting experiences together. Ending without a "why IB" hook.
MUST PREP Tell me about a time you led a team or project. Leadership ▼
What they're testing
Can you drive outcomes without direct authority? Do you know how to motivate people and manage up?
Strong story elements
  • A concrete deliverable with a deadline
  • A specific challenge you had to navigate (conflict, resource constraint, ambiguity)
  • Actions YOU took — not "we decided"
  • A quantifiable outcome (revenue, % improvement, recognition)
  • A reflection: what you'd do differently
Do
Show how you handled conflict within the team
Demonstrate you empowered others, not just delegated
Own the result — good or bad
Don't
Pick a group project where you all "worked together"
Take credit for the team's work without showing your role
Use a story with no clear outcome
HIGH Describe a time you showed initiative / went above and beyond. Leadership ▼
What they're testing
IB analysts work without supervision at 2am. They want to know you're self-directed and don't wait to be told what to do.
Good story types
Identified a problem nobody else noticed → fixed it without being asked. Delivered more than what was requested. Took on a task outside your role to help the team succeed. Proposed a new process or idea that was adopted.
MUST PREP Tell me about a time you had a conflict with a colleague / teammate. Teamwork ▼
What they're testing
Can you work with difficult people professionally? Banking teams are high-pressure — interviewers screen for people who manage conflict maturely.
Structure
Pick a real conflict (not "we disagreed on the font colour"). Show that you sought to understand the other person's perspective, approached it directly and professionally (not passive-aggressively), and reached a resolution that benefited the project. Don't make the other person the villain.
Do
Show emotional maturity and self-reflection
Demonstrate the conflict was resolved
Acknowledge what you could have done better
Don't
Badmouth the other person
Pick a story where you were clearly in the wrong
Pick a trivial disagreement that wasn't really a conflict
HIGH Tell me about a time you had to work with someone very different from you. Teamwork ▼
What they're testing
Cultural intelligence, empathy, adaptability. Diverse teams are the norm in IB — you'll work with people from different backgrounds, countries, and communication styles.
Strong angle
Focus on how you adapted your communication style or approach, not just that you "respected differences." Show a concrete example of adjusting your behaviour and the positive outcome.
MUST PREP Tell me about your greatest failure or biggest mistake. Challenge ▼
What they're testing
Self-awareness and resilience. They want to see you can admit real mistakes and learn from them — not spin a "weakness" into a strength.
Rules
  • Pick something real and meaningful — not "I worked too hard" or "I'm a perfectionist"
  • Own the failure fully — don't blame circumstances or others
  • Spend 40% of the answer on what you learned and changed
  • Show you applied the lesson in a subsequent situation
Do
Be genuinely vulnerable — it builds trust
Show concrete change in behaviour afterwards
End on the learning, not the failure
Don't
Use a "disguised strength" failure
Pick something career-ending or character-damaging
Blame external factors
HIGH Tell me about a time you worked under pressure or met a tight deadline. Challenge ▼
What they're testing
IB is a high-pressure environment. This is almost a litmus test: "Can you function at 2am with a pitch due at 6am?"
Strong story elements
Concrete timeline (e.g. 48-hour crunch). What you prioritised and why. How you managed your energy and focus. The outcome — delivered on time, result quality. Importantly: how you'd handle it even better next time.
MUST PREP What is your greatest strength and greatest weakness? Fit ▼
Strength
Pick something genuinely relevant to IB (analytical rigour, attention to detail, resilience, client communication). Prove it with a specific example — don't just assert it.
Weakness — the right approach
Pick a real weakness that is not a core job requirement. Show genuine self-awareness AND concrete steps you've taken to improve. The improvement journey is more important than the weakness itself.

Example: "I used to struggle with delegating — I'd redo work myself instead of coaching others. I realised this was a bottleneck, so I started doing structured check-ins before diving in myself, and deliberately held back even when I could have fixed it faster."
HIGH Where do you see yourself in 5–10 years? Fit ▼
What they're testing
Are you using IB as a stepping stone (PE/HF exit), or are you genuinely interested in a banking career? Neither is wrong, but your answer should be coherent and show you've thought about it.
Good answer
Near-term: become excellent at execution, build sector expertise, take on deal responsibility. Medium-term: VP/Director path or move to a buy-side role where you can apply deal judgment more directly. Be honest — interviewers have heard every version of this answer, and authenticity wins over the "perfect" answer.
MEDIUM Do you have any questions for me? Fit ▼
Why this matters
This is an opportunity to show intellectual curiosity and genuine interest in the person across from you. Never say "no, I think you've covered everything."
Strong questions
  • "What deal have you worked on that you found most intellectually challenging, and why?"
  • "What's something you wish you'd known before joining the group?"
  • "How has the team's deal mix shifted over the past few years — and where do you see it going?"
  • "What differentiates the analysts who really thrive here from those who struggle?"
Do
Ask about their personal experience
Ask something you genuinely want to know
Have 3–4 questions ready (some may get answered)
Don't
Ask about salary or hours
Ask anything easily Googleable
Ask more than 2–3 questions
