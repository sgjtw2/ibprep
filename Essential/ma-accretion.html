Accretion / Dilution — Core Logic
Pro Forma EPS = (Buyer NI + Target NI + Synergies – Financing Costs) / Pro Forma Diluted Shares Accretion = Pro Forma EPS > Standalone EPS Dilution = Pro Forma EPS < Standalone EPS
All-Stock Breakeven Rule
Accretive if: Buyer P/E > Target P/E (after premium) Dilutive if: Buyer P/E < Target P/E (after premium)
The "exchange ratio" in stock deals determines how many buyer shares the target gets. A higher exchange ratio = more dilution to buyer.
All-Cash Breakeven Rule
Accretive if: Target Earnings Yield > After-tax Cost of Debt i.e. (Target NI / Purchase Price) > Rd×(1–t)
Factors That Drive Accretion
High target earnings relative to price↑ accretive
Cost synergies (immediate)↑ accretive
Low cost of financing (low rates)↑ accretive
High acquisition premium paid↑ dilutive
Lots of new shares issued↑ dilutive
D&A step-up from PPA↑ dilutive

Breakeven Analysis
Breakeven synergies needed: = (Financing Cost – Target NI) / (1 – tax rate) For stock deal: breakeven when Pro Forma EPS = Standalone EPS
Always ask: "what synergies does management need to assume to justify this deal?" If the breakeven is unrealistically high, the deal is questionable.

Purchase Price Allocation (PPA)
Goodwill = Purchase Price – Fair Value of Net Identifiable Assets – Fair Value Write-ups (PP&E, Intangibles)
Step by Step
1. Determine purchase price
2. Identify all tangible assets & liabilities at fair value
3. Identify and value intangible assets (customer lists, IP, brand)
4. Write-up PP&E to fair value (if book value understated)
5. Residual = goodwill (not allocated to specific assets)
Under US GAAP, goodwill is not amortized but tested for impairment annually. Acquired intangibles with finite lives are amortized — this is the D&A step-up that hurts EPS.
Impact of PPA on Financials
PP&E write-up↑ D&A → ↓ NI
Intangible write-up (finite life)↑ Amort → ↓ NI
Inventory write-upOne-time COGS hit when sold → ↓ NI
Deferred revenue write-down↓ Revenue in Year 1
Goodwill (infinite life)No amortization (US GAAP)
Deferred tax liabilityCreated by write-ups → ↑ L
PPA step-ups are non-cash (after initial recognition) but reduce GAAP net income. That's why analysts often add back amortization when comparing M&A deals — they use "Cash EPS" or "Adjusted EPS."
Goodwill Impairment
When testedAnnually, or when trigger event
Trigger eventsStock price decline, revenue miss
ProcessCompare carrying value to fair value
If carrying value > fair valueWrite-down goodwill, record loss
P&L impactNon-cash impairment charge on IS

A goodwill impairment signals that the acquiring company overpaid — the acquired business is worth less than what was paid. It's a non-cash charge but is a significant negative signal to the market. Examples: AOL-Time Warner (2002), HP-Autonomy (2012).

Walk me through an accretion / dilution analysis. CORE▼

An accretion/dilution analysis measures the impact of an acquisition on the buyer's EPS.

Step 1: Calculate the buyer's standalone EPS (Net Income / Diluted Shares).

Step 2: Calculate pro forma combined net income — add buyer NI + target NI + after-tax synergies – after-tax financing costs (interest on debt or opportunity cost of cash used) – after-tax D&A step-up from PPA.

Step 3: Calculate pro forma diluted shares — buyer's existing shares + any new shares issued (in a stock deal).

Step 4: Pro Forma EPS = Pro Forma NI / Pro Forma Shares. Compare to buyer's standalone EPS. If higher, the deal is accretive; if lower, dilutive.

The key insight: accretive doesn't mean the deal is good — it just means EPS goes up. You can make any deal accretive by using enough leverage.

When is a stock deal accretive vs dilutive? CORE▼

In a stock deal with no synergies: accretive if the buyer's P/E is higher than the target's effective P/E (purchase price / target EPS).

Intuition: if you're a 20× P/E buyer and you buy a company at 15× P/E, you're getting more earnings per dollar of stock issued than you're giving away. The deal is accretive.

If you're a 15× P/E buyer acquiring a 20× P/E target (paying 20× for their earnings), the deal is dilutive — you're issuing more expensive shares to buy cheaper earnings.

In practice, synergies often bridge a dilutive deal to accretive. The breakeven analysis tells you how much in synergies you need.

What is goodwill and how does it affect a merger model? CONCEPT▼

Goodwill = Purchase Price – Fair Value of Net Identifiable Assets. It represents the intangible premium paid — brand, customer relationships, future synergies.

In the merger model: goodwill is created on the buyer's balance sheet at close. It is not amortized under US GAAP (tested for impairment annually instead). However, other acquired intangibles (customer lists, IP) with finite useful lives are amortized — this is the D&A step-up that reduces GAAP EPS post-acquisition.

Analysts often add back acquired intangible amortization when computing "adjusted EPS" to show the cash earnings power of the combined company.

Why would a company do a dilutive acquisition? HARD▼

Accretion/dilution is a short-term, single-year EPS metric. A deal can be dilutive in year 1 but create substantial long-term value.

  • Strategic rationale: acquiring capabilities, technology, or market access that can't be built organically
  • Revenue synergies: take time to materialise — the combined business may be transformatively more valuable in 3–5 years
  • Defensive rationale: blocking a competitor from acquiring a key asset
  • Scale benefits: market share or distribution gains that improve long-term unit economics

Example: many technology acquisitions are immediately dilutive (buying loss-making companies at high multiples) but strategically justified. Boards and investors focus on long-term value creation, not just Year 1 EPS.

What are the main regulatory considerations in M&A? CONCEPT▼

Antitrust / Competition: the primary risk. In the US, deals above a threshold must be filed with the FTC and DOJ under the Hart-Scott-Rodino (HSR) Act. Regulators assess whether the combined entity would harm competition. Remedies include divestitures of overlapping business units.

Foreign investment review: CFIUS (Committee on Foreign Investment in the US) reviews deals involving foreign acquirers for national security risks. FIRB in Australia, NSIB in UK, etc.

Sector-specific: financial services (Fed, OCC), media (FCC), defence (CFIUS). Certain industries have ownership restrictions.

Regulatory risk is priced into the deal spread — if markets expect regulatory challenges, the target trades at a bigger discount to the offer price.
