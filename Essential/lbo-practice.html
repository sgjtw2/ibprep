Paper LBO — 5-Step Framework
1
Calculate Entry Price & Sources/Uses
Determine total purchase price using the entry multiple. Split into debt and equity. Uses = purchase price + fees. Sources = debt tranches + sponsor equity.
Purchase Price = Entry EBITDA × Entry Multiple Debt = Purchase Price × Leverage (e.g. 5× EBITDA or 60%) Equity = Purchase Price – Debt
2
Build Simplified Income Statement → EBITDA at Exit
Project revenue and EBITDA over the hold period using the given growth rate. No need for a full 3-statement model in a paper LBO.
EBITDA Year N = Entry EBITDA × (1 + growth)^N
3
Calculate Debt Paydown
Estimate FCF each year. Use it to pay down debt. Assume all FCF goes to debt repayment unless told otherwise.
FCF = EBITDA × (1 – Cash Tax Rate) – CapEx – Δ NWC (Simplification: FCF ≈ EBITDA × FCF conversion rate) Debt at Exit = Entry Debt – Cumulative FCF
4
Calculate Exit Equity Value
Apply exit multiple to exit EBITDA. Subtract remaining debt to get equity proceeds.
Exit Enterprise Value = Exit EBITDA × Exit Multiple Exit Equity = Exit EV – Remaining Debt
5
Calculate Returns — MoM and IRR
MoM is equity at exit over equity invested. IRR is the annualised return. Use the quick IRR approximation table.
MoM = Exit Equity / Entry Equity IRR (5yr): 2× ≈ 15%, 2.5× ≈ 20%, 3× ≈ 25% IRR (3yr): 2× ≈ 26%, 2.5× ≈ 36%, 3× ≈ 44%

Case Prompt — SteadyCo

A PE firm is acquiring SteadyCo, a manufacturer of industrial fasteners. You have 10 minutes to complete a paper LBO and determine whether the deal meets the fund's return hurdles.

LTM Revenue$200M
LTM EBITDA Margin25%
Entry EV/EBITDA Multiple8.0×
Leverage4.0× EBITDA
Revenue Growth (annual)5%
EBITDA Margin (constant)25%
FCF Conversion (% of EBITDA)50%
Exit Multiple8.0×
Hold Period5 years
Step 1 — Entry
Sources & Uses
LTM EBITDA = $200M × 25% = $50M Purchase Price = $50M × 8.0× = $400M Entry Debt = $50M × 4.0× = $200M Entry Equity = $400M – $200M = $200M
Step 2 — EBITDA Growth
Project EBITDA to Year 5
Year 0 EBITDA = $50M Year 1 EBITDA = $50M × 1.05 = $52.5M Year 2 EBITDA = $52.5M × 1.05 = $55.1M Year 3 EBITDA = $55.1M × 1.05 = $57.9M Year 4 EBITDA = $57.9M × 1.05 = $60.8M Year 5 EBITDA = $60.8M × 1.05 ≈ $63.8M
Shortcut: $50M × (1.05)^5 = $50M × 1.276 ≈ $63.8M
Step 3 — Debt Paydown
Cumulative FCF over 5 Years
Annual FCF ≈ EBITDA × 50% (use avg EBITDA ≈ $57M for simplicity) Avg FCF/yr ≈ $57M × 50% = ~$28.5M Cumulative FCF (5yr) ≈ $28.5M × 5 = $142.5M Debt at Exit = $200M – $142.5M = $57.5M
Step 4 — Exit
Exit Equity Value
Exit EV = $63.8M × 8.0× = $510.4M Exit Equity = $510.4M – $57.5M ≈ $452.9M
Step 5 — Returns
MoM & IRR
MoM = $452.9M / $200M ≈ 2.26×
IRR ≈ 18% (between 15% at 2× and 20% at 2.5× for 5yr)
MoM
2.26×
IRR
~18%
Verdict
Below 20% hurdle

Key insight: Returns are below the typical 20% PE hurdle. The no-multiple-expansion scenario constrains upside — the deal is driven entirely by debt paydown and modest EBITDA growth. To improve returns: negotiate a lower entry multiple, improve FCF conversion, or find a path to margin expansion.

Case Prompt — GrowthTech

A growth-focused PE fund is considering acquiring GrowthTech, a vertical SaaS company serving logistics firms. Assess whether this deal clears the fund's 20% IRR hurdle.

LTM Revenue$100M
LTM EBITDA Margin20%
Entry EV/EBITDA Multiple12.0×
Leverage3.0× EBITDA
Revenue Growth (annual)15%
EBITDA Margin (expands to 30% by Yr 5)+2% / year
FCF Conversion (% of EBITDA)40%
Exit Multiple12.0×
Hold Period5 years
Step 1 — Entry
Sources & Uses
LTM EBITDA = $100M × 20% = $20M Purchase Price = $20M × 12.0× = $240M Entry Debt = $20M × 3.0× = $60M Entry Equity = $240M – $60M = $180M
Step 2 — EBITDA Growth
Project Revenue & EBITDA (margin expands)
Year 0: Rev $100M, Margin 20%, EBITDA $20M Year 1: Rev $115M, Margin 22%, EBITDA $25.3M Year 2: Rev $132M, Margin 24%, EBITDA $31.7M Year 3: Rev $152M, Margin 26%, EBITDA $39.5M Year 4: Rev $175M, Margin 28%, EBITDA $49.0M Year 5: Rev $201M, Margin 30%, EBITDA $60.3M
Step 3 — Debt Paydown
Avg EBITDA (Yr 1–5) ≈ $41M Avg FCF/yr ≈ $41M × 40% ≈ $16.4M Cumulative FCF ≈ $16.4M × 5 ≈ $82M Debt at Exit = $60M – $60M = $0M (fully paid down)
Low leverage + growing FCF → debt fully repaid by Yr 4-5
Step 4 — Exit
Exit EV = $60.3M × 12.0× = $723.6M Exit Equity = $723.6M – $0 ≈ $723.6M
Step 5 — Returns
MoM = $723.6M / $180M ≈ 4.0×
IRR ≈ 32% (well above 20% hurdle for 5yr at 4×)
MoM
4.0×
IRR
~32%
Verdict
Clears hurdle

Key insight: Returns are driven by EBITDA growth (revenue × margin expansion), not leverage. Low leverage means lower risk. The high entry multiple (12×) is justified by growth. Sensitivity: if exit multiple compresses to 10×, Exit EV = $603M, MoM ≈ 3.35×, IRR still ~27% — still a strong deal.

Case Prompt — TurnCo

TurnCo is a consumer products distributor with a cost structure that management believes can be significantly improved through operational changes. Assess the deal assuming the margin improvement plan succeeds.

LTM Revenue$500M
LTM EBITDA Margin10%
Entry EV/EBITDA Multiple7.0×
Leverage5.0× EBITDA
Revenue Growth (flat)0%
EBITDA Margin (improves linearly)10% → 18% over 5 yrs
FCF Conversion (% of EBITDA)55%
Exit Multiple8.0× (re-rating on improved margins)
Hold Period5 years
Step 1 — Entry
LTM EBITDA = $500M × 10% = $50M Purchase Price = $50M × 7.0× = $350M Entry Debt = $50M × 5.0× = $250M Entry Equity = $350M – $250M = $100M
Step 2 — EBITDA (margin expansion, flat rev)
Margin improves by +1.6%/yr (10% → 18% over 5yrs) Year 0: Rev $500M, Margin 10%, EBITDA $50M Year 1: Rev $500M, Margin 11.6%, EBITDA $58M Year 2: Rev $500M, Margin 13.2%, EBITDA $66M Year 3: Rev $500M, Margin 14.8%, EBITDA $74M Year 4: Rev $500M, Margin 16.4%, EBITDA $82M Year 5: Rev $500M, Margin 18.0%, EBITDA $90M
Step 3 — Debt Paydown
Avg EBITDA (Yr 1–5) ≈ $74M Avg FCF/yr ≈ $74M × 55% ≈ $40.7M Cumulative FCF ≈ $40.7M × 5 ≈ $203.5M Debt at Exit = $250M – $203.5M ≈ $46.5M
Step 4 — Exit (multiple expansion to 8×)
Exit EV = $90M × 8.0× = $720M Exit Equity = $720M – $46.5M ≈ $673.5M
Step 5 — Returns
MoM = $673.5M / $100M ≈ 6.7×
IRR ≈ 46% (exceptionally high — driven by low entry equity + multiple expansion)
MoM
6.7×
IRR
~46%
Verdict
Exceptional if thesis holds

Key insight: High leverage (5×) + low entry equity ($100M) creates massive equity upside — but also maximum downside risk if the margin thesis fails. If margins only improve to 14% (half the plan), EBITDA at exit = $70M, exit EV = $560M, equity ≈ $513M, MoM ≈ 5.1× — still excellent. The deal is resilient to partial failure because the entry equity base is small. This is classic operational turnaround PE logic.
