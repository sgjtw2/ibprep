Walk me through a DCF. CORE▼

A DCF values a company based on the present value of its future free cash flows. There are four steps:

1. Project unlevered FCF over a 5–10 year explicit forecast period. FCF = EBIT×(1–t) + D&A – CapEx – ΔNWC.

2. Calculate WACC — the blended cost of capital. Cost of equity via CAPM (Rf + β×ERP), cost of debt after-tax, weighted by capital structure.

3. Calculate terminal value — either Gordon Growth (FCF/(WACC–g)) or exit multiple (EBITDA × multiple). Discount it back to present value.

4. Sum the PV of FCFs and PV of terminal value to get enterprise value. Subtract net debt to get equity value, divide by diluted shares for implied price.

The key weakness is that terminal value typically represents 60–80% of total value, making the result very sensitive to WACC and growth rate assumptions.

What happens to a DCF if WACC increases? CORE▼

Implied value decreases. A higher WACC means future cash flows are discounted more heavily, reducing their present value. Both the PV of the projection period FCFs and the PV of terminal value fall.

The terminal value is especially sensitive because it uses WACC in the denominator: TV = FCF/(WACC–g). A 1% increase in WACC on a company with 2% terminal growth rate and 10% WACC changes the denominator from 8% to 9% — an 11% reduction in terminal value.

Why do precedent transactions trade at a premium to trading comps? CONCEPT▼

Two reasons: control premium and synergies.

Trading comps reflect minority, non-controlling interests — you're buying a share, not the whole company. When a buyer acquires a company outright, they gain control: the ability to change management, strategy, capital allocation. That control has value, so buyers pay a premium (typically 20–40%).

Strategic buyers also pay for synergies — cost savings (headcount reduction, procurement) and revenue synergies (cross-selling, distribution). These future benefits justify a higher price today.

PE buyers pay less than strategics because they don't realise revenue synergies — they rely on operational improvements and leverage.

How do you calculate WACC, and what are its components? CORE▼

WACC = (E/V)×Re + (D/V)×Rd×(1–t)

Cost of equity (Re) = Rf + β × ERP. The risk-free rate is the 10-year Treasury yield (~4.2% today). The equity risk premium is the excess return investors expect over risk-free (~5%). Beta measures systematic risk relative to the market.

Cost of debt (Rd) is the yield on the company's debt, tax-effected because interest is deductible.

Weights (E/V and D/V) should use market value of equity and debt, not book value. For a private company, you'd use a comparable public company's capital structure or the target capital structure.

Beta for private companies: unlever the beta of comparable public companies, then re-lever at the target's capital structure.

When would you NOT use a DCF? HARD▼

DCF is unreliable when:

  • Unprofitable / early-stage companies — negative FCF for many years makes terminal value dominate entirely
  • Highly cyclical businesses — normalisation of earnings is arbitrary
  • Financial companies (banks, insurance) — FCF is not meaningful; use DDM or P/Book instead
  • Companies in distress — going-concern assumptions break down; use liquidation or restructuring analysis
  • Resource companies (mining, oil) — use NAV, which models reserves explicitly

In practice, DCF is most reliable for mature, stable businesses with predictable FCF and a long operating history.

What is the difference between enterprise value and equity value? CORE▼

Enterprise value (EV) is the total value of the business — what you'd pay to buy 100% of the company, including its debt. It represents value to all capital providers (debt and equity holders).

Equity value is what remains for equity holders after debt is repaid. Equity Value = EV – Net Debt.

EV is capital-structure neutral, making it useful for comparing companies with different leverage. Most operating multiples (EV/EBITDA, EV/EBIT) use EV. Equity multiples (P/E) use equity value.

Key bridge: EV = Market Cap + Total Debt + Preferred Stock + Minority Interest – Cash & Equivalents.
