Walk me through the 3 financial statements. CORE ▼

The Income Statement shows profitability over a period. Starting from revenue, we deduct COGS to get gross profit, then subtract operating expenses and D&A to reach EBIT. After interest and taxes we arrive at net income.

The Balance Sheet is a snapshot at a point in time. Assets equal liabilities plus equity. Net income flows into retained earnings on the equity side, and ending cash from the CFS flows into the cash line on the asset side.

The Cash Flow Statement reconciles net income to actual cash. We start with net income, add back non-cash charges (D&A, SBC), adjust for working capital changes, then show cash from investing (CapEx) and financing (debt, dividends, buybacks). The ending cash balance matches the cash line on the balance sheet.

If D&A increases by $10, walk through the impact on all 3 statements. (Tax rate 40%) CORE ▼

Income Statement: EBIT falls $10 → taxes fall $4 (40%) → net income falls $6.

Cash Flow Statement: Net income –$6, but D&A add-back +$10 → CFO increases by $4 (the tax shield).

Balance Sheet: Cash +$4. PP&E –$10 (higher accumulated depreciation). Retained earnings –$6 (lower NI). Assets: net change = +$4 –$10 = –$6. Equity: –$6. Balanced ✓

Net cash benefit = $10 × tax rate = $10 × 40% = $4
If CapEx increases by $10, walk through all 3 statements. CORE ▼

Income Statement: No impact. CapEx is capitalized, not expensed. Net income unchanged.

Cash Flow Statement: CFI decreases by $10 (cash outflow in investing activities). Overall cash decreases by $10.

Balance Sheet: PP&E +$10, Cash –$10. Net asset change = zero (asset swap). Equity unchanged. Balanced ✓

Note: future periods will see higher D&A from this CapEx, which will then impact IS/CFS.

How do you go from net income to free cash flow? CONCEPT ▼
FCF = Net Income + D&A – CapEx ± Δ Working Capital

Or alternatively starting from EBIT:

UFCF = EBIT × (1 – Tax Rate) + D&A – CapEx – Δ NWC

The key adjustments: add back non-cash charges (D&A, SBC), subtract real cash investments (CapEx), and adjust for the cash tied up or released from working capital changes.

FCFF (unlevered) ignores capital structure — used in DCF. FCFE (levered) subtracts net debt repayment — used to value equity directly.

What is the difference between EBIT, EBITDA, and Net Income? CONCEPT ▼
Net Income → + Taxes + Interest Expense = EBIT → + D&A = EBITDA

EBITDA is a proxy for operating cash flow before capex. Used widely in valuation multiples (EV/EBITDA) because it strips out financing (interest), taxes, and non-cash charges, making it easier to compare across companies and capital structures.

EBIT reflects operating profitability after D&A — useful when D&A is a meaningful cost (e.g. capital-intensive industries).

Net Income is the bottom line after everything, but it's distorted by capital structure and tax differences — least useful for cross-company comparisons.

A company has $100 in revenue and 40% EBITDA margins. It is taxed at 30%. CapEx is $10 and D&A is $5. NWC increases by $3. What is FCF? HARD ▼
EBITDA = $100 × 40% = $40 EBIT = $40 – $5 = $35 NOPAT = $35 × (1 – 30%) = $24.5 FCF = $24.5 + $5 – $10 – $3 = $16.5

Starting from EBITDA: add back D&A to EBIT, tax-effect EBIT to get NOPAT, then add D&A back (non-cash), subtract CapEx (real cash), and subtract the NWC increase (cash tied up in operations).

If accounts receivable increases by $10, what happens to the 3 statements? CORE ▼

Income Statement: No impact. Revenue is already recognized (accrual basis), regardless of cash collection.

Cash Flow Statement: AR increase is a use of cash — CFO decreases by $10.

Balance Sheet: AR +$10 (current asset). Cash –$10. Net asset change = zero. Equity unchanged. Balanced ✓

Intuition: you've "earned" the revenue but haven't collected the cash yet. The cash flow statement reflects this timing difference.

Company issues $100 of debt and uses it to buy back $100 of stock. Walk through the impact. HARD ▼

Income Statement: No immediate impact (assume same period, no interest expense yet).

Cash Flow Statement: CFF: +$100 (debt issued), –$100 (buyback) = net zero cash change.

Balance Sheet: Debt +$100. Treasury Stock –$100 (equity). Cash unchanged. Assets unchanged. Total liabilities +$100, equity –$100 → balanced ✓

The key effect: leverage increases, equity decreases. In future periods, interest expense will reduce net income and retained earnings, creating a further shift from equity to liabilities.

What is goodwill and when is it created? CONCEPT ▼
Goodwill = Purchase Price – Fair Value of Net Identifiable Assets

Created in an acquisition when you pay more than the fair value of the target's net assets. It represents intangible value: brand, customer relationships, synergies.

Under US GAAP, goodwill is not amortized but is tested annually for impairment. Under IFRS, some intangibles are amortized.

In IB, interviewers test whether you understand that goodwill on the buyer's balance sheet is the "premium" paid — and that it must be justified by synergies in the deal model.

Why can a company have positive net income but negative cash flow? CONCEPT ▼

Several reasons:

  • Working capital build-up: revenue recognized but not collected (high AR), or inventory bought but not sold
  • Heavy CapEx: large capital expenditures flow through investing, not the IS
  • Debt repayment: financing outflows don't affect net income
  • Deferred revenue reversal: cash collected earlier, revenue recognized now

This is why CFO is often a better signal of business quality than net income — it's harder to manipulate.
